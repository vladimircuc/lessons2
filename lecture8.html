What this lecture adds

In Lecture 7 you learned to find text that fits a pattern, using match, search, findall, and finditer, with character classes, repetition, and anchors. This lecture assumes all of that and adds three new abilities:

  • Capture groups, which let you pull out specific pieces of a match instead of the whole thing.
  • Greedy versus non-greedy, which lets you control whether a pattern grabs as much as possible or as little as possible.
  • Substitution with re.sub, which finds matches and replaces them, so regex can transform text, not just locate it.

The promised explanation

Why we always write r"..."

Here is the thing we kept putting off. A backslash is already special inside a normal Python string. "\n" is not a backslash and an n, it is a single newline character. "\t" is a tab. "\b" is a backspace. Python turns those backslash combinations into special characters for you.

Regex also uses backslashes for its own shortcuts, like \d, \w, and \b. So when you write a pattern as a normal string, Python and regex fight over the backslash, and you get confusing bugs.

A raw string, written with an r in front, tells Python to leave the backslashes completely alone and pass them straight to regex. Run this to see the difference:

raw_strings.py

      

      
Why this matters
Look at \b. In regex it means "word boundary." But as a normal Python string, "\b" is a backspace character. So if you write a word-boundary pattern without the r, it silently turns into a backspace and your pattern quietly breaks. Writing r"\b" avoids the whole problem. The rule is simply: always put the r there.

Grabbing pieces

      
Capture groups

      
Often you do not want the whole match, you want a specific piece of it. Wrap that piece in parentheses ( ) and it becomes a capture group, a part you can pull out on its own. The full pattern still has to match, the parentheses just mark what you want to keep.

      

        
You callYou get

        
m.group()the whole match (same as group(0))

        
m.group(1)the text inside the first pair of parentheses

        
m.group(2)the text inside the second pair

        
m.groups()a tuple of all the captured pieces

      

      
(\d+)-(\d+)
"one or more digits, captured as group 1, then a dash, then one or more digits, captured as group 2." A pattern for something like a range, 12-34.

The part that surprises everyone

      
findall changes when you add groups

      
This is the single most important idea in this lecture. Normally findall returns the whole matches. But the moment your pattern has a capture group, findall returns only the captured pieces instead.

      
    
        
  • With one group, you get a list of strings, just the captured part of each match.
    • 
        
  • With two or more groups, you get a list of tuples, one tuple per match.
    • 
      

      

        
findall_groups.py

        
        

      

      
The takeaway
If findall gives you something you did not expect, check whether your pattern has parentheses. Parentheses quietly switch findall from "give me the whole matches" to "give me just the captured pieces."

How much to grab

      
Greedy versus non-greedy

      
Remember from Lecture 7 that repetition is greedy: + and * grab as many characters as they can. Usually that is fine, but sometimes it grabs way too much.

      
To make a repetition non-greedy (also called lazy), put a ? right after it. So +? means "one or more, but as few as possible," and *? means "zero or more, but as few as possible." The pattern still has to work, the engine just stops at the earliest point it can.

      
\(.+\)  versus  \(.+?\)
Both read as "an open paren, some characters, a close paren." The first is greedy and stretches to the LAST close paren it can reach. The second is lazy and stops at the FIRST close paren.

      

        
greedy.py

        
        

      

      
The greedy version saw the open paren at the start, then .+ swallowed everything, including the middle )(, all the way to the final paren, giving one big match. The lazy version stopped the moment it could close, so it found each group separately.

      
A second job for ?
This is the same ? you met in Lecture 7, but a different job. On its own, ? means "zero or one." Placed right after another repetition like + or *, it means "be lazy." Position is everything, just like the two jobs of ^.

Find and replace

Memorize Put this on your crib sheet

        
Always write patterns and replacements as raw strings, r"...", because a bare \b becomes a backspace.

        
Capture groups:

        
( )         marks a piece to capture
m.group(1)  the first captured piece
m.group(2)  the second
m.groups()  a tuple of all captured pieces

        
findall with groups (the big one):

        
no groups    -> list of whole matches
one group    -> list of the captured strings
2+ groups    -> list of tuples

        
Non-greedy: add ? after a repetition to grab as little as possible.

        
.+   greedy, grabs as much as it can
.+?  lazy,   grabs as little as it can
also *?  and  {m,n}?

        
Substitution and backreferences:

        
re.sub(pattern, replacement, text)  -> new string
\1 \2 in the replacement   reuse captured groups
\1 inside the pattern      "the same text again"

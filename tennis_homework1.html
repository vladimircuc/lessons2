Filtering

Boolean masks, single and combined conditions. Tasks 7 to 13.

7

Show every match won by Novak Djokovic. warm-up

Solution
df[df["winner_name"] == "Novak Djokovic"]
8

Show every match played on grass where the winner hit at least 15 aces. step up

Hint: two conditions joined with &, each in its own parentheses.

Solution
df[(df["surface"] == "Grass") & (df["w_ace"] >= 15)]
9

Show every match played on Clay or Grass. step up

Hint: .isin([...]) is cleaner than two OR conditions.

Solution
df[df["surface"].isin(["Clay", "Grass"])]
10

How many matches were not played on Hard courts? step up

Hint: you can compare with !=, or flip a mask with ~.

Solution
(df["surface"] != "Hard").sum()

This also counts the few matches with a missing surface as "not Hard". If you want strictly Clay or Grass, use df["surface"].isin(["Clay","Grass"]).sum().

Predictor prep

Building new columns and asking the kind of questions the match predictor will answer. Tasks 26 to 30.

26

Create a new column total_aces = winner aces + loser aces. Then show the 10 matches with the highest total_aces. step up

Solution
df["total_aces"] = df["w_ace"] + df["l_ace"]
df.nlargest(10, "total_aces")[["winner_name", "loser_name", "total_aces", "tourney_name"]]
27

Create a column rank_diff = loser_rank - winner_rank. In how many matches did the higher-ranked player win (rank_diff > 0)? Then compute what fraction that is of all matches where both rankings are known. challenge

Hint: df["rank_diff"].notna().sum() gives the number of matches where both ranks are known. Divide the two.

Solution
df["rank_diff"] = df["loser_rank"] - df["winner_rank"]

favorite_wins = (df["rank_diff"] > 0).sum()      # higher-ranked player won
known = df["rank_diff"].notna().sum()            # matches with both ranks present

print(favorite_wins)
print(favorite_wins / known)                     # fraction, e.g. about 0.65

That fraction is the accuracy of the simplest possible predictor, "the higher-ranked player wins". It is the number our model has to beat.

28

Create a column upset that is True when the winner was ranked worse than the loser (winner_rank > loser_rank). How many upsets happened, and which surface had the most? challenge

Hint: sum the True/False column for the count; filter to upsets then value_counts() the surface.

Solution
df["upset"] = df["winner_rank"] > df["loser_rank"]

print(df["upset"].sum())                            # how many upsets
print(df[df["upset"]]["surface"].value_counts())    # which surface had the most
29

Create a column ace_diff = w_ace - l_ace and print its average across all matches. Is it positive? In a comment, write one line on what that tells you about winners. challenge

Solution
df["ace_diff"] = df["w_ace"] - df["l_ace"]
df["ace_diff"].mean()
# positive average means winners tend to out-serve (out-ace) their opponents
30

For each player, compute how many matches they won and their average aces per win, then show the top 10 by wins. challenge

Hint: group by winner name and use .agg(["count", "mean"]) on the winner aces column, then sort by the count.

Solution
df.groupby("winner_name")["w_ace"].agg(["count", "mean"]).sort_values(
    "count", ascending=False
).head(10)
# count = matches won, mean = average aces per win

This little table is a first "player profile". Next week we expand it into the features the model learns from.
